   1. Entropy
       1. ***Statistics of the motions of the microscopic constituents of a system***
          1. Classical
          2. ***Quantum***
             1. *Use as buzzword*
       2. State variables depend only on the equilibrium condition, not on the path evolution to that state.
          1. State variables can be functions of state, also called [state functions](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function>), in a sense that one state variable is a [mathematical function](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)>) of other state variables. Often, if some properties of a system are determined, they are sufficient to determine the state of the system and thus other properties' values.
       3. The entropy change *dS of a system can be well-defined as a small portion of heat Î´Qrev transferred from the surroundings to the system during a reversible process divided by the temperature T of the system during this heat transfer.*
       4. [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#Statistical_mechanics](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#Statistical_mechanics>)
          1. The [interpretation of entropy in statistical mechanics](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(statistical_thermodynamics)>) is the measure of uncertainty, disorder, or *mixedupness* in the phrase of [Gibbs](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs>), which remains about a system after its observable macroscopic properties, such as temperature, pressure and volume, have been taken into account.
          2. For a given set of macroscopic variables, the entropy measures the degree to which the probability of the system is spread out over different possible [microstates](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microstate_(statistical_mechanics)>).
          3. In contrast to the macrostate, which characterizes plainly observable average quantities, a microstate specifies all molecular details about the system including the ***position and momentum of every molecule***. 
             1. The more such states are available to the system with appreciable probability, the greater the entropy.
             2. In statistical mechanics, entropy is a measure of the number of ways a system can be arranged, often taken to be a measure of "disorder" (the higher the entropy, the higher the disorder)
       5. The entropy of a system depends on its internal energy and its external parameters, such as its volume.
       6. In [chemical engineering](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_engineering>), the principles of thermodynamics are commonly applied to "[open systems](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory)>)", i.e. those in which heat, [work](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)>), and [mass](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass>) flow ***across the system boundary.***
       7. It is possible (in a thermal context) to regard lower entropy as a measure of the *effectiveness* or *usefulness* of a particular quantity of energy.
       8. When viewed in terms of [information theory](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory>), the entropy state function is the amount of information in the system that is needed to fully specify the microstate of the system.
          1. [Entropy](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory)>) is the measure of the amount of missing information before reception.[\[84\]](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-85>) Often called *Shannon entropy*, it was originally devised by [Claude Shannon](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon>) in 1948 to study the size of information of a transmitted message.
       9. Entropy is the only quantity in the physical sciences that seems to imply a particular direction of progress, sometimes called an [arrow of time](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow_of_time>). As time progresses, the second law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of an [isolated system](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system>) never decreases in large systems over significant periods of time. Hence, from this perspective, entropy measurement is thought of as a clock in these conditions.[\[99\]](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-100>) Since the 19th century, a number the philosophers have drawn upon the concept of entropy to develop novel metaphysical and ethical systems.
      10. via [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pessimism#Entropy_pessimism](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pessimism#Entropy_pessimism>)
          1.
