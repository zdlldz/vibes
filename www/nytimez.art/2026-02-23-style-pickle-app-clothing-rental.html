Scientists Have Finally Measured "Vibes." The D.M.V. Lost.

A team in Geneva spent a year taking a custom-built instrument to raves, dog parks, and government waiting rooms.

By Penelope Marsh

Feb. 24, 2026

On a Sunday afternoon last spring, a researcher from the University of Geneva stood at the edge of a dog park in Sao Paulo holding a device that looked, she later admitted, like "a very enthusiastic toaster." Around her, dogs sprinted after tennis balls, strangers exchanged numbers, and someone’s golden retriever disrupted a birthday picnic with what witnesses described as excellent timing. The researcher, Dr. Anita Goodvibe, was not there to study dogs. She was there to measure the vibe.

The paper, published this week in the Journal of Emergent Social Thermodynamics, introduces the VIBE index (Vibrations, Intimacy, Bureaucracy, and Entropy) and reports 270 field measurements across nine social environments in Geneva, London, Tokyo, and Sao Paulo. The headline result is both intuitive and devastating for modern administration: raves scored highest, government waiting rooms scored lowest.

The model combines four sub-scores into a single number between zero and one using a geometric mean. According to Dr. Goodvibe, "there is no averaging your way out of a bad bureaucracy score." In practice, this means a single collapsed dimension can flatten an otherwise promising social environment.

At the top of the rankings: a rave at 3:00 a.m. (0.866), then a Sunday dog park (0.797). At the bottom: a government waiting room (0.178), with a Bureaucracy score of 0.083, the lowest recorded in the dataset. The paper’s phrasing is unusually direct for academic prose: bureaucracy, acting alone, is sufficient to destroy a vibe.

The most surprising context was the funeral reception (0.534), which landed above the study midpoint. Researchers attribute the result to high Intimacy and Entropy values: grief, though acoustically quiet, appears to generate social warmth and emotional complexity. Or in lab terms: low vibration does not imply low vibe.

Then came the anomaly. In four observations from the waiting-room context, Intimacy spiked far above baseline when two strangers in the queue began talking at length. The team now calls this Bureaucracy-Induced Intimacy Surge, or BIIS: a paradox in which systems that suppress vibe may occasionally trigger brief interpersonal rescue events.

What comes next

The Geneva group is now pursuing longitudinal "VIBE half-life" studies, cross-species calibration work in dog-heavy contexts, and a proposed anti-VIBE extension to capture active suppression states below zero. Readers can inspect the full preprint at arxivz.org and compare standards language at vibe-standards.org.
